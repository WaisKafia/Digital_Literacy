Casting Nets in a Digital Ocean: Empowering Bangladesh’s Fishermen with Online Safety

Bridging the gap between traditional marine life and the digital world. A community-led initiative supported by Monash University.

From PhD Research to Community Action

This project was born from the fieldwork findings of a PhD project at the Faculty of Information Technology, Monash University. Our research examined the information culture of small-scale marine fishing communities on an island in Bangladesh and the factors shaping how they engage with digital information.

Supported by the Equity, Diversity, and Inclusion (EDI) Fund, we have moved beyond research to practical application. We provide the tools necessary for these communities to navigate the "digital shopping mall" safely, ensuring they can enjoy the benefits of the internet without falling victim to its hidden dangers.

Image: Research Fieldwork

The Challenge: Why Digital Literacy Matters Here

The internet is full of opportunities, but for our fishing communities, it also presents unique threats.

1. Financial & Impersonation Scams

Fishermen are increasingly targeted by "spoofing" methods where scammers pretend to be banks or delivery services. Common threats include fake "Bkash" account suspension warnings and "Inheritance Offers" that demand upfront fees.

2. The Romance Scam Epidemic

Scammers exploit the community's openness by building emotional bonds over weeks or months before asking for money for "emergencies". We teach the community that if they haven't met face-to-face, they must not send money.

3. Misinformation & Health Myths

In a close-knit community, false context spreads fast. A photo of a doctor at a Union Parishad health camp can be manipulated to create a fake story. We fight "Imposter Content" where fake government logos are used to promise non-existent relief.

Community Context: Life on the Island

How Information Flows

In this small-scale marine fishing community, information has traditionally flowed through physical sources: neighbors, the Sardar, the Imam, and local tea shops. Today, digital sources are mixing with these traditional voices.

The Digital Anchor

To bridge this gap, our program encourages the identification of local "digital anchors"—trusted individuals within the community who can help others authenticate digital information and understand online content.

Recent Training Events

15 Jan 2026

Workshop: "The Algorithmic Net"

Goal: Helping fishermen understand that algorithms are not people, but patterns based on actions.

Key Takeaway: Participants learned that what they click influences what they see, helping them avoid "algorithmic polarization" where news feeds reinforce biases.

20 Dec 2025

Seminar: Protecting Our Families

Focus: Gaming Addiction and Child Safety.

The Issue: Many community members reported losing sleep or failing to finish tasks due to online gaming.

The Solution: We introduced strategies for "digital detox" and taught parents how to talk to children about misinformation.
